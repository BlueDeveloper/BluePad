How to Write Math Equations in Markdown with KaTeX (Beginner's Guide)

June 29, 2026 · BluePad Team · 6 min read

If you have ever tried to write a fraction, an exponent, or a summation symbol in a plain text note, you know how quickly it falls apart. You end up with something like x^2 + (a/b) and hope the reader can decode it. For homework, lecture notes, technical docs, or research write-ups, that is not good enough — math needs to actually look like math.

The good news is that you do not need a heavyweight equation editor. With KaTeX, you write equations in a compact LaTeX-style syntax right inside your Markdown file, and the editor renders them as clean, typeset formulas. Write x^2 and you see a proper superscript; write a fraction and you get a real stacked fraction with a horizontal bar.

What Is KaTeX?

KaTeX is an open-source math typesetting library, originally built by Khan Academy, that renders LaTeX math notation into clean formulas. LaTeX has been the standard for writing mathematics in academia for decades, and KaTeX brings a large, practical subset of that syntax to the web and to Markdown editors.

The key idea is the same one behind Markdown itself: you write in plain, readable text, and the tool handles the visual formatting. You type a short description of the equation using familiar commands, and KaTeX draws the typeset result. Because it focuses on rendering quickly, math appears almost instantly as you write rather than after a delay.

For Markdown users this matters because the equation lives inside the same plain-text file as the rest of your writing. It stays searchable, copy-pasteable, and version-control friendly — there is no separate image to manage or regenerate when a number changes.

Inline Math vs Block Math

There are two ways to place math in Markdown, and the difference is simply where the equation sits relative to your text.

Inline math sits inside a sentence and is wrapped in single dollar signs. It is perfect for referring to a variable or a small expression mid-paragraph:

The area of a circle is $A = \pi r^2$, where $r$ is the radius.

Block math stands on its own line, centered, and is wrapped in double dollar signs. Use it for important equations you want to highlight:

$$ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 $$

That is the entire system. Single dollars keep an expression in the flow of a sentence; double dollars give it its own line and a bit more breathing room.

Writing Common Equations

LaTeX math syntax looks intimidating at first, but a handful of commands cover the vast majority of everyday equations. Here are the ones worth memorizing first.

What you want What you type
Exponent / superscript x^2
Subscript x_1
Fraction \frac{a}{b}
Square root \sqrt{x}
Greek letters \alpha, \beta, \pi, \theta
Summation \sum_{i=1}^{n}
Integral \int_{a}^{b}

Two rules unlock most of this. First, the caret (^) raises and the underscore (_) lowers — so x^2 is a square and x_1 is a subscript. Second, anything longer than a single character goes inside curly braces. To write x to the tenth power you need x^{10}, not x^10, otherwise only the 1 gets raised.

Putting a few pieces together, here is a complete block equation — the quadratic formula:

$$ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} $$

Tips for Readable Math

A few small habits keep your equations clean and easy to fix later.

  • Always brace multi-character groups. Use x^{n+1} and a_{ij} so the whole group is raised or lowered, not just the first character.
  • Use \left( and \right) for tall brackets. They automatically grow to fit fractions or roots inside them, so the parentheses do not look tiny next to a big expression.
  • Add spacing with \, or \quad. KaTeX ignores most plain spaces in math mode, so use these commands when you want a visible gap.
  • Keep one equation per block. If a derivation has several steps, separate them rather than cramming everything into a single line.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to know LaTeX to use KaTeX?

Not really. You only need a small set of commands — superscripts, subscripts, fractions, roots, and Greek letters cover most everyday math. You can keep a short cheat sheet nearby and pick up the rest as you go. Full LaTeX is a much larger system, but KaTeX math syntax is the approachable core of it.

Does KaTeX support every LaTeX command?

KaTeX supports a large and practical subset of LaTeX math, which covers the vast majority of common equations. A small number of niche or highly specialized commands are not included, but for notes, homework, and technical documentation you are very unlikely to hit a wall.

Will my equations show up everywhere my Markdown does?

Only in tools that support math rendering. Some platforms render $...$ and $$...$$ automatically, while plain text viewers will just show the raw syntax. To see live equations as you type, use an editor with built-in KaTeX support so what you write renders instantly.
